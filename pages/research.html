PhD Thesis SSHRC Funded ($20k)

Disaggregating Disparity: Understanding Eating Disorders Among Bisexual Women in Canada

Supervisors: Dr. Jennifer Lane & Dr. Megan Aston
The Scope

Eating disorders affect millions of Canadians, yet bisexual women are understudied and experience some of the worst health outcomes within 2S/LGBTQ communities. This project builds foundational knowledge about bisexual women’s experiences with eating disorders and care in Canada to inform more inclusive, equitable practice - especially in nursing and community settings.

Findings will help clinicians and community organizations improve assessment, referral, and treatment pathways for bisexual women - reducing underdiagnosis, tailoring supports, and strengthening culturally safe, trauma‑informed care.

Research Aims
  • Maps the Evidence: A JBI‑guided scoping review to understand how research reports on bisexual women with eating disorders and where the gaps are.
  • Combines Data & Voices:A convergent parallel mixed‑methods study that analyzes survey data (including experiences of size discrimination and access to care) alongside interviews with bisexual women across Canada.
  • Centres EquityUses a feminist poststructuralist lens to examine how power, stigma, and systems shape care, and to generate practical, context‑sensitive recommendations.

__ Methodological Framework ________
Constructivist Grounded Theory

My work is rooted in Charmaz’s Constructivist Grounded Theory (CGT). I do not view data as something to be "mined," but as a co-construction between researcher and participant. This approach allows me to build theories that are deeply grounded in the lived realities of marginalized women, rather than imposing existing clinical frameworks upon them.

Intersectionality & Structural Competence

Health disparities cannot be understood in isolation. I utilize an intersectional lens to examine how systems of power (sexism, biphobia, weight stigma) overlap to create unique barriers to care. My research aims to move beyond "cultural competency" toward "structural competence"—recognizing that patient health is shaped by upstream social and political forces.

Arts-Based Knowledge Mobilization

Traditional academic papers often fail to reach the communities they study. I employ arts-based methods—such as graphic medicine and visual storytelling—to translate complex qualitative findings into accessible, impactful formats for patients, clinicians, and the public.
